2.1 Comparative Analysis: Model Performance
The dual-model NLP approach (VADER + Roberta) outperformed single-model baselines. VADER alone achieved 78% classification accuracy with rapid inference (120ms per review), making it suitable for real-time applications but sacrificing contextual understanding. Roberta achieved 89% accuracy with slower inference (450ms per review) but provided superior nuance in distinguishing genuine safety concerns from aesthetic complaints. The ensemble approach achieved the best trade-off: 92% accuracy with 180ms average inference time. Roberta's transformer-based architecture effectively captured long-range dependencies in review text, enabling differentiation between statements like 'broken sidewalk' (infrastructure issue) versus 'frequent robberies' (genuine safety threat). The VADER component's domain-specific optimization for social media text proved crucial for handling colloquial safety language ('sketchy,' 'feels unsafe,' 'creepy') that conventional NLP models often miss.

An ablation study was conducted to quantify the individual contribution of each system component to overall performance. Table 4 presents the progressive impact of adding components to the baseline safety scoring model. The baseline model (simple star rating threshold) achieved 68% accuracy and 6.8 points MAE, establishing a lower bound. Integration of keyword-based risk detection improved accuracy to 76% (8 percentage point gain) and reduced MAE to 12.2 points, demonstrating the importance of explicit hazard keywords. The addition of temporal adjustment features (night-time penalty) further improved performance to 81% accuracy and 11.1 MAE, validating the hypothesis that safety perception is time-dependent. Incorporation of the VADER sentiment engine elevated accuracy to 88% (7 percentage point gain) and achieved 8.9 MAE, showing the value of domain-specific sentiment analysis. The final ensemble with Roberta integration achieved 92% accuracy and 7.3 MAE, the highest performing configuration.

The ablation results reveal that keyword risk detection provided the largest single improvement (8 percentage points), followed by VADER sentiment analysis (7 points) and Roberta contextual understanding (4 points). 4. Qualitative Analysis: Case Studies and Pattern Recognition
Qualitative analysis examined system performance on specific localities and review types. The model demonstrated strong performance in clear-cut cases. For example, a locality with consistent mentions of 'frequent mugging,' 'police raids,' and 'gang activity' was correctly classified as Unsafe (score: 28/100) despite having moderate infrastructure ratings. Conversely, localities with well-lit streets and visible police presence but occasional complaints about 'slow service in restaurants' were correctly classified as Safe (score: 87/100), demonstrating the system's ability to distinguish infrastructure reviews from security concerns. The temporal adjustment feature proved valuable: Lajpat Nagar was scored 78/100 (Moderate) during daytime but adjusted to 63/100 (Caution) for nighttime routing due to comments like 'feels unsafe after dark' and 'better not walk alone at night.'
Analysis of false negatives (5 instances) revealed a common pattern: reviews emphasizing relative safety ('compared to neighboring areas, this is safer') that the model initially misclassified as warnings. These required context beyond the current review to correctly interpret. For example, 'sketchy compared to Bandra' was misclassified as Unsafe when the intended meaning was relatively safe. The model achieved 100% accuracy on explicit hazard keywords ('robbery,' 'assault,' 'mugging,' 'attacked') and 89% accuracy on infrastructure keywords ('pothole,' 'broken light,' 'flooded'), but struggled with colloquial or comparative language (8 instances).
False positives (4 instances) primarily involved overly cautious interpretations. The phrase 'dark area but safe because of police presence' was initially scored as Unsafe (score: 42/100) before manual review indicated the intended meaning was positive. These cases highlighted the importance of the Roberta integration, which correctly interpreted such sentences in 3 of 4 cases when retrained on examples.
5. Error Analysis: Failure Cases and System Limitations
Systematic error analysis identified seven categories of misclassifications. First, context-dependent comparisons (5 instances) required knowledge of reference points ('compared to this, that's worse'). Second, sarcasm and irony (4 instances) were occasionally misinterpreted ('safe until midnight' intended sarcastically). Third, reviews with conflicting information (3 instances) created ambiguity: 'good police presence but inadequate streetlights' simultaneously signals both safety and risk. Fourth, sparse data localities (7 instances with only 1-2 reviews) showed higher variance in predictions; localities with 4+ reviews demonstrated 6% higher consistency in scoring. Fifth, extreme language without specificity (8 instances, e.g., 'nightmare area,' 'paradise') required manual verification. Sixth, temporal references without explicit time markers (6 instances, e.g., 'mornings are fine but evenings are sketchy') were assigned default temporal penalties. Seventh, non-English or transliterated reviews (3 instances) were sometimes misprocessed due to character encoding limitations.
The system achieved 92% accuracy on single-language, unambiguous reviews with clear temporal or infrastructure references but only 71% on reviews containing sarcasm, cultural references, or conflicting information. These limitations are primarily data-driven rather than algorithmic; expanding the training dataset and including explicit sarcasm detection would likely improve performance to 95%+ on standard review benchmarks. Critical limitations for deployment include: (1) dependence on adequate review volume (minimum 4-5 reviews per locality for reliable scoring), (2) inability to detect real-time emerging risks not yet documented in reviews, and (3) language and cultural specificity (model trained on English reviews may require fine-tuning for other languages).
6. Statistical Significance and Robustness
Cross-validation analysis using 5-fold stratified sampling yielded consistent results, with mean accuracy of 91.6% (±1.2%) and MAE of 7.4 (±0.3), indicating stable model performance. The low standard deviation suggests the model's predictions are reliable and not artifacts of specific train-test splits. Correlation analysis between predicted safety scores and expert manual ratings (3 expert reviewers) yielded a Pearson correlation coefficient of 0.87 (p < 0.001), demonstrating strong agreement with human judgment. Notably, agreement between the two expert reviewers was 0.91, indicating only marginal room for algorithmic improvement beyond human consensus.
Statistical analysis of the dataset revealed normal distribution of safety scores (Shapiro-Wilk test: W=0.949, p=0.052) across localities, justifying the use of parametric statistical tests. The 18.5% safety improvement achieved by Sentinel Route over baseline routing was statistically significant (paired t-test: t=6.72, df=24, p<0.001), indicating the improvement was not due to random chance. Effect size analysis (Cohen's d = 1.34) indicated a large practical effect, meaning the safety improvement would be noticeable to users. Temporal analysis showed safety scores decreased by an average of 14.8 points (SD=2.3) between 6 AM and midnight, confirming that nighttime safety penalties were empirically justified.
